Web Resources

Main

  1. CSS-Tricks
    CSS-Tricks is a blogsite dedicated to articles on CSS.

    I'm not much for simply reading articles. Usually, I better integrate what I learn by doing it.
    However, CSS-Tricks offers professional articles from real web developers, and as beginner,
    it's advisable to listen to the advice of more experienced individuals.

    I think one of the most important parts in the process of learning isn't memorization,
    but simply knowing that something exists. I can't remember the syntax of every command in CSS,
    but I can remember what to look up in order to find it again.

    Because of that, it's beneficial simply learn that something exists, even if you don't have a use for
    it yet. I think CSS-Tricks is great for that. Simply read an article or two a day and, over time,
    your knowledge of what is and isn't possible will accumulate.

    Rating: 7/10

  2. Stack Overflow
    Stack Overflow is an online forum dedicated to debugging code.

    It's a classic. Any time spent past five minutes of coding will have you needing to Google things.
    And anyone who's spent past five minutes Googling their code issues will stumble upon a Stack
    Overflow post on it.

    It's hard to say you've ever truly programmed if you haven't at least once needed to scour
    obscure Stack Overflow posts, melting your brain onto the screen trying to understand
    the esoteric nerd-speak of geeks who far surpass you.

    It's a classic, but of course, as any classic does, it gets scrutinized over the years.
    Common complains have to do with lack of accessibility, and outright hostility by answerers.
    Newcomers to coding naturally have to learn what their forebearers already know by heart.

    And so because of that, there is a LOT of repetition, which ends up irritating
    the volunteers who take the time out of their day to answer questions.
    It ends up that Stack Overflow is a harsh, but vital resource for the programmer.

    Rating: 8/10

  3. Dev.to
    A social site for web developers.

    Even for the stereotypically solitary programmer, networking is important.
    Programming is a vast discipline, and web design not much less than it.
    Due to that, a coder's best resource is often a second eye.

    Dev.to serves as a social site for developers. It aesthetically takes after Slack,
    yet functionally serves like a midway between Reddit and a news article's comments section.
    Not my favorite look, and there's horrendous margins wasted on the sides, but for a
    social site, it looks and works well enough.

    Of course, sites aren't really made by their makers, they're made by their users.
    And Dev.to offers plenty of people to connect with (over a million, in fact).
    And as a developer-specific site, all of its content and moderation are tailored to it.

    Rating: 7/10

  4. Templated.co
    Templates for CSS and HTML

    CSS and HTML have been around for decades, and in that time similar designs
    have been redone many times. In programming, it's best not to reinvent
    the wheel if it's already somewhere out there. And especially for those
    less experienced, it's best to work with designs made by experts.

    Templated.co offers ready-made designs to be used, speeding up the
    process of creating many of the necessary widgets and functionality found
    on most sites.

    Rating: 8/10

  5. Youtube
    An excellent resource for tutorial videos

    Anytime I'm learning a new programming language, I'll watch an introductory video
    on it. Any time I'm learning a new data structure, I'll watch an introductory
    video on it.

    Youtube is an excellent resource for finding tutorial videos and introductory
    explanations for concepts. You could pay for a class, but why do so when there's
    so many free videos that already exist?

    Rating: 9/10
